Low-Cost Pediatric Prosthetics

Affordable 3D-printed prosthetic limbs for children in Blacksburg, Virginia, built with a Virginia Tech engineering team.

Overview

Pediatric prosthetics are expensive and quickly outgrown. Children can need a new device every 12–18 months as they grow, and many families cannot afford repeated replacements. This project set out to design functional, low-cost upper-limb prosthetics using 3D printing — making them fast to produce, easy to customize, and affordable enough to replace as needed.

Challenge

The core tension was between cost and function. Cheap prosthetics tend to be cosmetic-only. Functional ones with grip mechanisms tend to be complex and costly. We needed to find a sweet spot: a device that was genuinely useful for a child's daily activities, could be printed on consumer-grade hardware, and cost under $50 in materials per unit.

Approach

  • Surveyed existing open-source prosthetic designs (e-NABLE, OpenBionics) and identified common failure points: poor wrist articulation, heavy weight, and uncomfortable sockets.
  • Developed a modular CAD model in SolidWorks that separated the socket, wrist, and finger mechanism — allowing each to be sized independently for different children.
  • Tested multiple FDM materials (PLA, PETG, TPU) for strength, flexibility, and comfort. Settled on a PETG frame with TPU grip surfaces.
  • Built a tensioning system using elastic cord that allows passive grip via wrist flexion — no motors, no batteries.
  • Fitted and tested prototypes with child participants under IRB-approved protocols.

Outcome

The final design cost approximately $35 in materials per unit and could be printed and assembled in under 8 hours. Three devices were fitted to children in the Blacksburg area during the project period. Feedback from families was positive, particularly around the weight (under 200g) and the ability to quickly reprint components as children grew.

Reflection

Working directly with the families and children who would use these devices changed the way I think about engineering. The best technical solution means nothing if it doesn't work in someone's actual life. Comfort, appearance, and ease of use mattered just as much as mechanical performance — and in some cases, more.
