OPM-MEG Infant Helmet

A wearable neuroimaging helmet enabling the first-ever OPM-MEG data collection in awake, behaving infants.

Overview

Optically pumped magnetometer-based magnetoencephalography (OPM-MEG) is a next-generation neuroimaging technique that measures magnetic fields produced by neural activity. Unlike traditional MEG systems, OPM sensors don't require cryogenic cooling, making wearable designs possible. This project focused on creating the first helmet designed specifically for infant participants — a critical step toward understanding early brain development.

Challenge

Infants present unique engineering constraints: their heads are small and variable in size, they move constantly, and any device must be extremely lightweight and safe. Existing adult OPM-MEG helmets could not be scaled down — the sensor geometry, weight distribution, and retention mechanism all needed to be fundamentally rethought.

Approach

  • Collected anthropometric data on infant head dimensions from published norms and 3D scanning of head models.
  • Designed a parametric CAD model in SolidWorks that could accommodate sensor placement patterns optimized for cortical coverage in infants.
  • Iterated on retention mechanisms to ensure a secure, comfortable fit during natural infant movement — without rigid chin straps or adhesives.
  • Fabricated prototypes using FDM and SLA 3D printing, testing weight, balance, and sensor seating with each iteration.
  • Collaborated with neuroscience researchers to validate sensor positioning against expected signal-to-noise requirements.

Outcome

The final helmet design successfully enabled the first OPM-MEG recordings in awake, behaving infants. The device weighed under 400g, maintained sensor contact across a range of infant head sizes, and was tolerated during sessions lasting up to 15 minutes. The data collected contributed to ongoing research into early auditory and visual processing in infant populations.

Reflection

This project taught me that the hardest engineering problems are often the ones with the most human constraints. Designing for infants required me to think beyond technical specifications and consider comfort, safety, and the reality that your end user can't give you verbal feedback. Close collaboration with the research team was essential — every design decision was validated against real experimental needs.
